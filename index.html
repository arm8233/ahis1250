How the Distant Past Can Help us Face the Climate Crisis

Lessons from ancient Egypt, ritual purity and the limits of archaeology
Ali Reza Malik

Bushfires in Tasmania (Matt Palmer)

The severity of human-induced climate change is increasingly visible to us. “It is worse, much worse, than you think," writes David Wallace-Wells in his book, The Uninhabitable Earth. Perhaps Wallace-Wells' dire warning isn't so alarmist, after all. Last year, France was beset with wildfires whose intensity is unprecedented. Unusually intense rainfalls caused floods and bushfires in Australia, with more unpredictable weather patterns to come in the future. Other parts of the world aren't faring any better. As prospects of a catastrophic climate change loom over us, set to upend our sense of normality in the near-future, we should look into the distant past through archaeology for lessons that can help us face future uncertainties.

Environmental crises, then and now

Archaeology is said to be the study of the human past. Whilst that is true, it is as much about the past as it is about the present and the future. When we look at the past, we see that ancient societies are not so different from ours, some in ways that we might not expect. After all, they were people just like us, with aspirations, dreams and fallibilities. They faced many of the same predicaments that we do today, be they pandemics, wars, food scarcity, conflicts or natural disasters, and responded to them in various ways. Disruptions to normal climate patterns further exacerbate these issues; this is equally true in our time as it was in the distant past, particularly in ancient Egypt.1


1 Nguyen, 2021

“Climate change is a stressor that can exacerbate issues, but it can also catalyse societal resilience"

Ancient Egyptian society had to grapple with climatic disruptions. During the First Intermediate Period, a time of political change and division the annual Nile floods that nourish riverside soil for agriculture were unusually low.2 Although recent archaeological research shows that the period was likely not the chaotic and dark time as it was portrayed by earlier academic works, with some even challenging the direct relation between climate change and collapse3, there is evidence to believe that the collapse of the centralised Old Kingdom was, at least, influenced by climatic disruptions.

2 Bunbury and Rowe, 2021, p. 20
3 García, 2021

Faiyum lake (Abdelrahman Ismail)

These disruptions posed existential threats to many ancient civilisations. A multidisciplinary research project focussing on the relationship between volcanic eruptions, political stability and state capacity of the Ptolemaic state of ancient Egypt seeks to understand how natural disasters and climatic disruptions can play a role in triggering conflicts and destabilising the political and economic landscapes.


Climate change is a stressor that can exacerbate issues, but it can also catalyse societal resilience. The disruptions to the Nile's flood level prompted ancient Egyptians to start measuring and recording flood heights, in addition to implementing adaptive measures.4 What we can learn is that adaptation and resilience-building are important to anticipate the unpredictability of climate change in our time.

4 Bunbury and Rowe, 2021, p. 21

Ritual purity and water scarcity

It's a truism that water is life, but its role in ancient Egyptian ritual can help us appreciate this fact even more. We can turn to the Book of the Dead to see how water is linked to ritual, whether ancient or modern.



The river Nile, seen from space (USGS)

When we think of a 'book,' we usually imagine a bunch of papers neatly bound by a cover.5 The Book of the Dead is not a 'book' in this modern sense, but rather a genre of funerary papyri found in ancient Egyptian tombs.6 These papyri served a purpose for the deceased in Pharaonic times: a manual to safely navigate the Duat, the netherworld.

5 Scalf, 2017, p. 21
6 ibid.

One spell in the Book, Spell 125 specifically, contains a declaration of the deceased's purity: “[…] I am pure. My purity is the purity of that great Bennu which is in the city of Suten-henen.”7 The concept of purity was important to the ancient Egyptians. Priests used water to purify themselves before entering the temples.8 Spells with reference to water were recited for purifying the Pharaoh.9 The role of water in maintaining purity in the ancient Egyptian religion highlights the importance of clean water access.

7 Book of the Dead, 125
8 Quack, 2013, p. 127
9 ibid., p. 130

Just as clean water was central to ancient Egyptian ritual, it remains important to the rituals of present-day religions10. Baha'is and Muslims rely on pure water to perform their ablutions before the daily obligatory prayers. Baptism, which relies on water, initiates a person into many Christian denominations.

10 Stelzle, 2022

However, a warming world means more difficult access to clean freshwater as rising sea levels makes groundwater more saline and erratic rainfall patterns diminish water tables in some regions. As the threat of water scarcity becomes more pronounced in the future, the role of water in ancient and present-day religions can perhaps inspire us to take a more meaningful climate action today.


Limits of archaeology

All things considered, we must acknowledge the limitations of archaeology. We may seem fairly confident about something in the past, but the truth is we may never know with certainty. Our ways of knowing about the past may be informed by modern biases, or we may impose modern interpretations on the past, thereby skewing the reality of what things really were and how they were perceived by ancient societies. This is the limitation of archaeology. As Paul Bahn11 says, everything in archaeology "is tentative, nothing is final".


11 2012, p. 7

Nevertheless, the knowledge that archaeology has produced is invaluable to us and especially pertinent to the challenge of our times. It allows us to study the impact that a changing climate has had and continues to have on human societies throughout a massive time span12, from the distant past and into the distant future.

12 Fagan and Durrani, 2020, p. 26

To acknowledge the limits of archaeology means that we must also acknowledge the limits of ongoing research into climate change, which may be currently insufficient to account for the future effects of a warming world.13

13 Lyon et al., 2021

What now?

What we can learn from studying the past is that we shouldn't underestimate the threats of climate change. We know that climatic disruptions pose stress on issues and can trigger a series of famine, conflicts and wars. In the past, climate and changing environments may have helped bring about the weakening and collapse of ancient polities.

These threats are equally, perhaps even more, true today. Although the sheer scale of anthropogenic climate change is incomparable to the climatic disruptions that many ancient societies faced the lessons of resilience and adaptation in the face of a threat from these societies can help us face climate change.

This is why archaeology has never been more important today. Archaeology is a nexus between our time and the past. It uncovers issues common to us and those who came before us, despite its limitations and potential biases, allowing us to learn from their dynamics over long periods of time.

The path ahead of us seems daunting, but studying how climate change affected ancient societies and how they responded to the challenges of their times, through ingenuity and mistakes alike, can help us understand and face those of our time.
